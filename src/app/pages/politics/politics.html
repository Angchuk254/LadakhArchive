Overview

A Land Governed from Afar — Fighting for Self-Rule

Ladakh's political story is one of persistent marginalization and resolute resistance. For over 190 years — from the Dogra conquest of 1834 to the present day — Ladakhis have been governed by powers seated far away: first Jammu, then Srinagar, and now Delhi. The demand for self-governance is not new; it is the defining thread of Ladakh's modern political identity.

Today, Ladakh stands at a historic crossroads. The 2019 reorganization granted UT status but stripped away existing protections without replacing them. The 6th Schedule movement represents the latest chapter in a 200-year quest: the right of a tribal people to govern their own land, protect their identity, and shape their own future.

How the Hill Council Works

The LAHDC has 26 elected councillors and 4 nominated members (including women and minority representatives). It has powers over local development, taxation, public health, education, agriculture, and minor forests. However, key areas — policing, major infrastructure, environmental regulation, and inter-state matters — remain with the Lt. Governor and central government.

Key limitation: The LAHDC cannot legislate on land, employment protection, or environmental regulation — the exact areas where Ladakhis feel most vulnerable after Article 370's abrogation. This is why the 6th Schedule demand is not just about autonomy — it's about survival.
